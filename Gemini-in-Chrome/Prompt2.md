To discover or design room-temperature superconductors and ultra-high-efficiency supercapacitors, quantum mechanics researchers focus heavily on low-dimensional, highly customized materials. [1, 2, 3] 
Instead of searching for random elements, scientists use quantum modeling—like Density Functional Theory (DFT)—to manipulate material structures at the atomic level. [4, 5] 
------------------------------
## 1. Superconductor Candidates
To achieve superconductivity at or near room temperature, materials must allow electrons to overcome their natural repulsion and form "Cooper pairs" that flow without friction. Scientists are tackling this through three primary frontiers: [6, 7, 8] 
## Carbon-Based "Twisted" Materials

* Twisted Bilayer Graphene: When two sheets of [graphene are stacked and twisted to a precise "magic angle" of $1.1^\circ$](https://www.quantamagazine.org/exotic-new-superconductors-delight-and-confound-20241206/), the electronic bands flatten. This slows down electrons and forces them to interact strongly, unlocking superconductivity.
* Transition Metal Dichalcogenides (TMDs): Following the success of graphene, atom-thick combinations of transition metals and chalcogens (like molybdenum disulfide) are being stacked in twisted arrangements to trigger unconventional superconductivity at warmer baselines. [9, 10, 11, 12, 13] 

## Superhydrides (High-Pressure Compounds)

* Lutetium-Nitrogen-Hydrogen (Lu-N-H): Compounds involving rare earth metals combined with hydrogen and nitrogen have demonstrated [superconducting capabilities at room temperature ($21^\circ\text{C}$ / $70^\circ\text{F}$)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Q7e42qFYpJs), though they still require extreme compression (~10,000 atmospheres) to stabilize.
* Pressure-Quenched Compounds: Researchers are using techniques like the "[pressure-quench protocol](https://www.uh.edu/news-events/stories/2025/february/02102025-tcsuh-breakthrough.php)" on complex mercury-copper-oxides ($\text{Hg-1223}$) to lock high-pressure superconducting states into place, attempting to keep them active even when returned to normal room pressure. [14, 15, 16] 

## Unconventional Ceramics & Minerals

* Nickelates: Structurally similar to the classic high-temperature cuprates (copper-oxides), [nickelates are a primary focus of quantum metallurgy](https://phys.org/news/2026-03-room-temperature-superconductor-scientists-agenda.html) because their electron behaviors can be easily tweaked via chemical doping.
* Miassite ($\text{Rh}_{17}\text{S}_{15}$): Discovered by the [Ames National Laboratory](https://www.ameslab.gov/news/scientists-reveal-the-first-unconventional-superconductor-that-can-be-found-in-mineral-form-in), this is one of the very few natural minerals found to act as an unconventional superconductor. Scientists study its unique structure to map out cheaper, more stable lab-grown alternatives. [17, 18, 19, 20, 21]

## 2. Quantum Supercapacitor Candidates
Traditional supercapacitors rely entirely on the physical surface area of carbon. Quantum supercapacitors, however, exploit Quantum Capacitance ($C_Q$)—the ability of a low-dimensional material to store energy because of its electronic density of states (the number of available quantum states for electrons to occupy). [5, 22, 23] 
## Heteroatom-Doped Graphene

* Nitrogen and Boron Doping: Pristine graphene actually has poor quantum capacitance because it lacks available electron states near its Fermi level. By systematically replacing carbon atoms with nitrogen, boron, or phosphorus, scientists create [local quantum defects that drastically increase the energy storage density](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/00268976.2024.2437531?src=). [4, 5, 24, 25, 26] 

## Graphene / Transition Metal Oxide (TMO) Composites [27, 28] 

* Nickel Oxide ($\text{NiO}$) & Cobalt Oxide ($\text{Co}_3\text{O}_4$) Coatings: Depositing nanoscale transition metal oxides onto a graphene framework merges two storage methods. The graphene provides highly conductive paths, while the TMO introduces "pseudocapacitance" (fast, battery-like quantum electron-transfer reactions), vastly scaling up overall capacity. [4, 25, 29] 

## Carbon Nanotube-Polymer Fibers

* SWCNT-Polyaniline Matrices: Integrating Single-Walled Carbon Nanotubes (SWCNTs) with conductive polymers like polyaniline creates flexible, film-like materials. The polymer stores massive amounts of charge via quantum states, while the carbon nanotube framework keeps the system structurally sound over [hundreds of thousands of rapid cycles](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=reLj79iUz60). [22] 

## Metal-Organic Frameworks (MOFs)

* Carbon-Free MOFs: Pioneered by [MIT researchers](https://news.mit.edu/2016/supercapacitor-made-without-carbon-1010), these materials bypass carbon entirely. They use sponge-like, conductive molecular networks made of metal ions and organic ligands. Quantum mechanics allows engineers to tune the pore sizes down to the angstrom level, matching the exact size of electrolyte ions for maximum energy capture. [23, 30]
